This week, I have been developing my Excel skills in preparation for the Excel MO-200 Associate exam; I’ve been following handy tutorials by the Youtube Channel [It's Matt](https://youtube.com/@itsmatt228?si=Z3l-_zMha2QEpHEf), along with an introduction to the tabs and ribbons of Excel in depth by the channel [Technology for Teachers and Students](https://youtube.com/@techteachersandstudents?si=znWvjwAPoE20eSZ3). I’ve found that their pace is easy to keep up with, and I find the latter tutorials are great to *learn* the actual Excel interface, whilst the former are useful to learn *how* to use the interface. 

Learning Excel as one of my first programs as a budding bioinformaticist may seem an interesting choice. Admittedly, learning Excel has been a personal goal of mine for a while; I want to use it in order to learn how to record, manage and organise data. Whilst it is simple enough at entry-level, I am interested in fully unpicking the ins and outs of this program. And, I like its versatility - its role in analysing financial data seems especially useful in tracking my own finances! Despite its differences to typical bioinformatics programs, I hope that in learning Excel, I can ultimately use that knowledge to create my own projects to enrich my data management in my personal life. I hope to share my interesting Excel finds and applications over the coming months. 😊

On top of that, I have spent this week learning about the applications of Git, GitHub, and GitHub pages, following the detailed [Git course](https://www.codecademy.com/enrolled/courses/learn-git) over on Codecademy. I think it’ll still take me time to wrap my head around Git’s extensive branching and repository system, but perhaps the best way to put my skills to the test is to actually practice them. Hence, the launch of this blog! This week, I’ve tried experimenting with changing the interface of the blog a little - I’ve added a handy ‘Projects’ and 'Vidoes' tab for me to upload my future projects as and when I complete them, so watch this space!👀

Finally, in terms of my ongoing research in my lab, I have been looking at virus sequence analysis, specifically Covid-19 strains. I sourced the strains using the NCBI virus database; then, performed multiple sequence alignment using MAFFT using the Fasta files from NCBI. I then used Jalview for alignment visualisation, IQ-Tree for tree construction, and then iTol for tree visualisation! It was interesting to learn how to use these programs, and I'm intrigued to explore them further - we will see where it goes!
